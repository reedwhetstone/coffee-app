## Thesis

Citrini's viral "2028 Global Intelligence Crisis" report claims AI will hollow out the SaaS economy. They're half right. AI does compress the value of software-as-product. But they're modeling adoption rates like a tech demo, not an enterprise procurement cycle. The real insight isn't that SaaS dies. It's that the moat shifts: from software (increasingly replicable) to data (increasingly not) and from subscription pricing (pay for access) to work-output pricing (pay for results).

## Structure

### Citrini Is Right About One Thing (300 words)
The core claim: AI compresses the value of software-as-product. A competent developer + Claude Code can replicate mid-market SaaS core functionality in weeks. That procurement conversation ("what if we just built this ourselves?") is real. Citrini isn't wrong about the mechanism. They're wrong about the speed.

Key data point: Citrini envisions this playing out by 2028. Citadel's rebuttal shows software engineer jobs are up 11% YoY and AI adoption for daily work is "unexpectedly stable." The gap between "technically possible" and "actually adopted at enterprise scale" is measured in years, not quarters. People still run businesses on fax machines.

### The Stickiness Problem Citrini Ignores (350 words)
Enterprise SaaS has <1% monthly churn. That's not because the software is irreplaceable. It's because switching costs are enormous: data migration, workflow retraining, integration dependencies, compliance certification, institutional knowledge embedded in custom configs.

Citrini models SaaS disruption like consumer app switching. Enterprise procurement doesn't work that way. The Fortune 500 procurement manager in their own anecdote didn't cancel; he renewed at a 30% discount. That's the actual dynamic: pricing pressure, not replacement.

What AI WILL replace: the "dinky stuff." Small efficiency tools, single-purpose apps, anything that isn't mission critical and doesn't require regulatory compliance. The long tail of $50-200/mo SaaS that companies bought because building it wasn't worth the effort. Now building it is trivial. That market compresses. Enterprise systems of record? Those are a different beast.

### Where the Moat Actually Moves (400 words)
If software is increasingly replicable, what's left?

**Data.** Netflix's value isn't its streaming UI (replicable). It's the discography, the recommendation engine trained on billions of viewing hours, the content library. Facebook's value isn't the feed algorithm. It's the social graph. Purveyors' value isn't the web app. It's the normalized coffee catalog across 14 suppliers that nobody else has.

Data moats deepen with time. Every day the scraper runs, the dataset gets richer. Every user interaction adds signal. Software can be rebuilt in weeks. A proprietary dataset built over years cannot.

**Work-output pricing.** The subscription model breaks when AI can do the seat's work. Why pay per-seat for a customer support tool when you could pay per-resolved-ticket? The shift from "pay for access" to "pay for outcomes" realigns incentives and creates a new kind of moat: whoever can demonstrate measurable results keeps the contract. This is where AI-native companies have an advantage over incumbents who are bolting AI onto seat-based pricing.

### The Real Gap (300 words)
The story Citrini should be telling isn't about SaaS collapse. It's about the gap between businesses that leverage AI and those that don't. That gap is widening right now, and it's where the actual economic disruption happens in the near term.

A company running AI-assisted development, AI-driven data pipelines, and work-output pricing is operating at a fundamentally different velocity than one still buying $500k/yr SaaS renewals and managing 200-person dev teams. But the disruption isn't "the second company dies." It's "the second company pays more for worse outcomes, slowly, over years." That's how enterprise technology transitions actually work. Not overnight collapse. Gradual margin compression.

"It's never as bad or as good as they say." Citrini's scenario is a useful stress test but a terrible forecast. The moats are shifting, not vanishing.

### Closing (150 words)
Brief. What this means for builders: build on data, not just software. Price on outcomes, not access. The software layer is increasingly commodity. The intelligence layer, the proprietary data, the domain expertise encoded in your pipeline, that's what compounds. Everything else is a feature that can be replicated in a sprint.

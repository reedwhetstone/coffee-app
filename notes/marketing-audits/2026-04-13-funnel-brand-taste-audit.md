# Funnel and brand taste audit, April 13, 2026

## Overall verdict

Strong enough to merge, with real taste debt still visible.

Overall marketing and brand excellence rating: **7.8/10**.

The direction is materially better than the older, blurrier "one membership blob" story. The funnel now has a real shape: public buyer path first, then clearer product routes for workflow, API, analytics, and enterprise. That is the right strategic move. The remaining issues are not fatal architecture failures, but they do still soften trust and polish at key decision points.

## What improved materially

### 1. The top-of-funnel is finally honest

The homepage hero and CTA now push people into the public catalog first instead of prematurely forcing account creation. That is better product marketing and better funnel taste. It lets the product prove value before asking for commitment.

### 2. The product family structure is much clearer

The split between **Mallard Studio**, **Parchment API**, **Parchment Intelligence**, and **Enterprise** is a big improvement. It creates a category map instead of making the buyer reverse-engineer the company from mixed feature lists.

### 3. Subscription messaging is much more adult

The subscription page now behaves like a pricing and account-state surface, not a vague pricing wall. Separating owned state by product family is good product marketing because it reduces ambiguity after purchase too, not just before purchase.

### 4. API branding is substantially stronger

The move to **Parchment API** and **Parchment Console** is a real upgrade. The API page feels more like a credible developer product page and less like an internal route dump. It better signals product intent, especially for technical buyers.

### 5. Analytics is closer to a market-intelligence product

The analytics page now presents a much more legible value ladder: some public proof, more detail after sign-in, premium depth for intelligence buyers. That is a far better monetization and trust pattern than over-gating everything or over-giving everything away.

## Where the funnel is still weak

### 1. The brand architecture is clearer, but not fully settled

There are now several names in play: **Purveyors**, **Mallard Studio**, **Parchment API**, **Parchment Intelligence**, **Parchment Console**, and in some places older labels like **Roaster+**, **Explorer**, or role-derived language. The direction is right, but the naming system still feels partially transitional rather than fully composed.

This is not just style. Transitional naming weakens recall and buyer confidence.

### 2. Self-serve versus contact-sales boundaries are still not crisp enough

The broad structure is correct, but the edges still feel fuzzy:

- API messaging references self-serve evaluation and paid production tiers, but some surfaces still lean on internal entitlement language.
- Intelligence is positioned as premium analytics, but the exact self-serve offer versus future packaging still feels slightly in motion.
- Enterprise is correctly contact-sales, but the line between premium self-serve and enterprise customization could be stated even more cleanly.

A strong funnel should make the upgrade path feel inevitable, not interpretive.

### 3. Some copy still sounds like internal cleanup language, not polished product language

Examples include phrasing like:

- "pricing and account-state surface"
- "not a generic membership blob"
- "resolved billing and entitlement state"
- "status-only sections for now"

Those are useful implementation truths, but they should be mostly invisible to buyers. They read like migration scaffolding leaking into the product.

### 4. The homepage product chooser is smart, but slightly overexplained

The move from a simple pricing table to product-path cards is correct. But some of the copy still explains the system from the company’s perspective rather than the buyer’s perspective. It occasionally feels like taxonomy before desire.

The best product pages make the buyer think, "yes, that is my tool," not "I understand your packaging model."

### 5. Analytics has value, but the premium story is not yet maximally sharp

The analytics page is much better, but the product promise still leans more on access structure than on unmistakable business outcomes. It proves there is data. It is less strong at proving why a buyer should specifically pay for the intelligence layer versus simply enjoy the free market view.

## Surface-by-surface assessment

### Homepage, nav, pricing

**Verdict: good strategic correction, not fully premium-polished yet.**

What works:

- Public catalog first is the right lead motion.
- Nav is more funnel-aware.
- Product-family pricing cards are a major clarity improvement.
- CTA flow is less manipulative and more coherent.

What still drags:

- "For Buyers" is directionally right, but slightly generic as nav language.
- Some product descriptions are still functional instead of magnetic.
- The four-card product architecture is smart, but the naming system around it still needs one more tightening pass.

### Subscription surface

**Verdict: structurally strong, copy still a bit too operational.**

What works:

- Clear separation of product families.
- Stronger management model for accounts that own multiple products.
- Honest handling of self-serve versus enterprise.
- Better post-purchase clarity.

What still drags:

- Too much backend truth is visible in user-facing language.
- Some headlines are good category labels but not strong demand generators.
- The page is more coherent than exciting.

### Analytics surface

**Verdict: credible and useful, but premium differentiation can sharpen.**

What works:

- Good free-to-paid ladder.
- Real value is visible before gating.
- "Market intelligence" positioning is much stronger than generic analytics language.
- The page feels like a product surface, not just a dashboard.

What still drags:

- Premium benefit framing is still slightly abstract.
- The distinction between public market visibility and paid intelligence could be more outcome-led.
- Some gating copy sounds product-managerial rather than brand-confident.

### API marketing and console

**Verdict: strongest surface in the set.**

What works:

- Parchment naming significantly improves seriousness.
- API page has a believable developer-product tone.
- Console naming and structure feel cleaner and more productized.
- The free evaluation to paid production ladder is intuitive.

What still drags:

- A few places still expose internal tier-to-role mapping more than ideal.
- Some plan naming across API surfaces still feels partly transitional.
- The promise is good, but a bit more proof or trust framing would improve conversion confidence.

### Category clarity

**Good.**
Buyers can now understand that there is a workflow product, a data product, an intelligence product, and an enterprise path.

### Product hierarchy clarity

**Good, with some naming residue.**
The structure is much better, but the language system is not fully normalized yet.

### CTA honesty and conversion flow coherence

**Very good.**
This is one of the biggest wins. The product now asks for less trust upfront and earns more of it.

### Self-serve versus contact-sales boundary clarity

**Pretty good, not excellent yet.**
Mostly right, but still slightly fuzzy around premium analytics and some API tier messaging.

### Consistency of naming and terminology

**Fair to good.**
Improved a lot, but still the weakest strategic area left.

### Buyer confidence, trust, credibility

**Good.**
Much stronger than before, especially on API and analytics. Some migration-ish copy still hurts polish.

### Overall brand taste and polish

**Good, not yet elite.**
The company now looks like it has a product system. It does not yet look like every message was edited by the same ruthless brand brain.

## Top strengths

1. **The funnel now respects buyer intent.** Public proof first, account and payment later.
2. **Product family clarity is real.** Workflow, API, intelligence, enterprise is a sellable structure.
3. **API branding is excellent relative to the rest.** Parchment API and Parchment Console are strong names.
4. **The subscription page is much more trustworthy.** It explains what the customer owns without collapsing everything into one confused plan.
5. **Analytics now supports monetization logic.** Free value exists, premium depth exists, and the ladder is legible.

## Top weaknesses

1. **Brand architecture is still transitional.** Too many names and tier labels still coexist imperfectly.
2. **Some user-facing copy leaks implementation context.** That reduces elegance and buyer trust.
3. **Premium analytics positioning is not yet maximally outcome-driven.**
4. **Some labels explain the system rather than sell the benefit.**
5. **The overall voice is coherent enough, but not yet unmistakably high-taste.**

## Must-fix before merge

I do **not** see a blocker-level messaging problem that should stop these PRs from merging.

If one thing must be addressed before merge, it would be this only:

1. **Remove or soften obviously internal/product-ops phrasing on public-facing pricing/subscription surfaces.**
   - Phrases like "generic membership blob," "resolved billing and entitlement state," and "status-only sections for now" should not survive if they are visible to normal buyers.
   - This is the one area that most clearly makes the product feel mid-migration instead of fully intentional.

If that cleanup is already constrained by scope, I would still merge, but I would log it as immediate follow-up.

## Post-merge improvements

1. **Finish the naming system.**

   - Decide the final hierarchy between company brand, app brand, API brand, intelligence brand, and console brand.
   - Eliminate leftover legacy or entitlement-derived naming from user-visible surfaces.

2. **Sharpen premium analytics around outcomes, not access levels.**

   - Move from "unlock more views" toward "make better sourcing decisions faster" and similar buyer-value framing.

3. **Tighten self-serve versus sales-led language.**

   - Especially on API and intelligence surfaces, make the boundaries unmistakable.

4. **Rewrite product-card copy to be more buyer-native.**

   - Less taxonomy, more job-to-be-done, payoff, and who-it-is-for language.

5. **Add more trust proof where possible.**
   - Examples: supplier coverage proof, freshness proof, usage trust cues, clearer social/operational credibility.

## Merge recommendation

**Mergeable.**

These changes materially improve the funnel and the company’s market-facing coherence. The remaining problems are mostly polish and message refinement, not structural confusion. The work clears the bar for merge, and it meaningfully upgrades the brand and conversion story. The next pass should be about tightening language and finishing the naming system, not rethinking the architecture.
